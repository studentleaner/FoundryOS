# Probability Assignment Methodology

Point-scores are deterministic and highly misleading for early-stage ventures. An idea scoring a 7.2/10 does not mean "it has a 72% chance of success." 

To model real asymmetric risk, the Strategic Intelligence Engine (SIE) fundamentally converts composite point-scores into probability distributions. 

---

## 1. Categorizing Uncertainty

Not all uncertainties are equal. Uncertainties are bucketed into three types:
- **Type A: Market Uncertainty** (Adoption speeds, pricing acceptance)
- **Type B: Competitive Uncertainty** (Clone speed, incumbent reaction)
- **Type C: Execution & Capital Uncertainty** (Burn rate variability, regulatory shocks)

## 2. The 5 Core Distribution Variables

Instead of arbitrary percentages, the SIE derives strict probabilities generated by the Multi-Agent Debate.

1. **Market Adoption Velocity (MAV):** Low / Medium / High
   - *Driver:* Distribution Agent Score & Market Feasibility.
2. **Competitor Reaction Intensity (CRI):** None / Moderate / Aggressive
   - *Driver:* Competitor Threat Engine (CTSF) & Strategic Overlap.
3. **Burn Rate Variability (BRV):** Optimized / Expected / Inefficient
   - *Driver:* Capital Efficiency Agent.
4. **Commoditization Speed (CS):** Slow (>36mo) / Moderate (18-36mo) / Fast (<18mo)
   - *Driver:* Commoditization Agent & Clone Speed Score.
5. **Regulatory Shock (RS):** Stable / Tightening / Disruptive
   - *Driver:* Risk Agent.

---

## 3. Confidence Flattening & Tail Risk Injection

### Confidence Flattening
If the aggregate Confidence Level from the Multi-Agent Debate is low (e.g., < 0.6), the system automatically flattens the probability distributions. This means adjusting tight bell curves toward a completely uniform distribution, mathematically enforcing "We do not know what will happen" and preventing overconfidence bias.

### Tail Risk Injection (Black Swans)
Institutional-grade engines must model catastrophic outlier events. The SIE systematically injects a baseline 5–10% probability of extreme tail risks, such as:
- A total funding market freeze.
- A fatal regulatory ban on the core technology.
- A sudden platform policy change (e.g., Apple blocks the API).

These distributions are then fed directly into the Monte Carlo Scenario Simulator.
